Albert Einstein:
Albert Einstein was a theoretical physicist best known for developing the theory of relativity, which revolutionized our understanding of space, time, and gravity. His equation, E=mcÂ², laid the groundwork for modern physics and contributed significantly to the development of quantum mechanics.

Marie Curie:
Marie Curie was a pioneering physicist and chemist who conducted groundbreaking research on radioactivity. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize and remains the only person to have won Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields: Physics and Chemistry.

Martin Luther King Jr.:
Martin Luther King Jr. was an influential civil rights leader who championed nonviolent resistance to achieve racial equality in the United States. His leadership during the Montgomery Bus Boycott and his powerful speeches, including the famous "I Have a Dream" speech, were instrumental in advancing civil rights.

Mahatma Gandhi:
Mahatma Gandhi was an Indian leader who employed nonviolent civil disobedience to lead India to independence from British rule. His philosophy of nonviolence and peaceful protest has inspired civil rights movements worldwide.

Nelson Mandela:
Nelson Mandela was a South African anti-apartheid revolutionary who became the country's first black president. Imprisoned for 27 years for his activism, Mandela's leadership and reconciliation efforts helped dismantle apartheid and foster a new era of peace and democracy in South Africa.

Frida Kahlo:
Frida Kahlo was a Mexican painter known for her vivid and surreal self-portraits that explored themes of identity, pain, and the human body. Her work has gained international acclaim for its emotional intensity and unique style, making her an iconic figure in art history.

Steve Jobs:
Steve Jobs was a visionary entrepreneur and co-founder of Apple Inc. His innovative approach to technology and design revolutionized personal computing, music, and mobile phones with products like the Macintosh, iPod, iPhone, and iPad, leaving a lasting impact on the tech industry.

Malala Yousafzai:
Malala Yousafzai is a Pakistani education activist and the youngest-ever Nobel Prize laureate. She survived a Taliban assassination attempt and has since become a global advocate for girls' education, co-authoring the memoir "I Am Malala" and founding the Malala Fund.

Leonardo da Vinci:
Leonardo da Vinci was a Renaissance polymath whose contributions spanned art, science, engineering, anatomy, and more. Renowned for masterpieces like "The Last Supper" and "Mona Lisa," his inventive mind also conceived ideas for flying machines and other technological marvels.

Oprah Winfrey:
Oprah Winfrey is a media mogul, talk show host, actress, and philanthropist. Her talk show, "The Oprah Winfrey Show," became one of the highest-rated programs in television history. Oprah's influence extends beyond television, with significant contributions to literature, education, and social issues.
